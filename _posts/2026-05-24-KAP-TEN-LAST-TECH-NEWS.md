# Top 10 Tech News of the Week

Welcome to this week's top 10 tech rundown, where we dive into the latest and greatest in the world of technology. From groundbreaking AI announcements to nostalgic looks back at iconic brands, we've got a lot to cover. Let’s get started!

## 1. France's New Internet Safety Law: A Comprehensive Approach

This week, France unveiled a new set of measures aimed at making the internet a safer place, emphasizing consumer protection and combating online fraud and harassment. This ambitious plan, comprising around twenty different measures, builds upon the European Union's Digital Services Act (DSA) and Digital Markets Act (DMA), which seek to curb the market power of tech giants and ensure fairer digital practices. The core idea is to protect vulnerable users from online scams, with a particular focus on filtering out malicious content and websites.

The upcoming 2024 Olympic Games are already a hotbed for fraudulent activities, such as the sale of fake tickets, making this initiative particularly timely. A test version of the anti-scam filter will be rolled out in September during the Rugby World Cup, serving as a critical pilot before broader implementation. While some experts express skepticism about the effectiveness of blacklists (given how quickly malicious sites can reappear under new names), this represents a significant first step in the fight against cybercrime.

## 2. The Anti-Scam Filter: How It Works and Its Challenges

One of the cornerstone measures of France's new digital policy is the "anti-scam filter." This filter is designed to display a warning message, likely in the form of a pop-up, whenever an internet user encounters a suspicious website, email, or SMS message. The goal is to provide immediate alerts about potential threats, such as phishing attempts, malware, or fraudulent offers. To implement this, internet service providers will be tasked with creating and regularly updating a blacklist of dangerous sites, largely based on user reports. The CNIL (France's national data protection authority) will oversee these efforts, ensuring compliance and effectiveness.

However, the concept of an anti-scam filter isn't without its critics. Concerns have been raised about the potential for false positives, the difficulty of maintaining an exhaustive and up-to-date blacklist, and the sheer volume of new malicious content that appears daily. Despite these challenges, the filter is seen as a proactive measure to raise awareness and provide a layer of defense for unsuspecting users, especially those less familiar with online dangers. The success of this filter will largely depend on its ability to evolve rapidly and adapt to new threats.

## 3. Combating Online Hate and Harassment: Banning Cyberbullies

Beyond financial scams, the French government’s new law also targets online hate speech and cyberharassment. In addition to existing fines, the legislation introduces a "banning" penalty for individuals found guilty of spreading hateful content, including violent images, child pornography, incitement to terrorism, homophobia, transphobia, or Holocaust denial. This provision aims to block offenders from accessing online platforms for periods ranging from six months to a year. This move signifies a stronger stance against harmful online behavior, attempting to create a more positive and respectful digital environment.

A key challenge with this measure lies in enforcement. While platforms will be responsible for implementing these bans and face fines of up to 75,000 euros for non-compliance, the ease with which users can create new accounts using different email addresses or phone numbers remains a concern. The idea of blocking IP addresses could also disproportionately affect entire households. The details of how platforms will effectively prevent circumvention of these bans, while respecting user privacy, are still being worked out. This highlights the complex balance between freedom of speech, user moderation, and enforcing digital civility.

## 4. AI in Public Services: A Chatbot for French Administration

In a fascinating development, France is set to integrate artificial intelligence into its public services. Beginning in September, 200 "France Services" agents will pilot a chatbot,
similar to ChatGPT, designed to assist with administrative queries. The objective is to provide quicker and more efficient responses to citizens' questions, streamlining administrative
processes and improving overall user experience. This initiative signals a broader trend of leveraging AI to modernize public administration and enhance accessibility.

The project prioritizes data confidentiality, ensuring that sensitive user information remains secure. A preliminary assessment of the chatbot's effectiveness is scheduled for February 2024. The primary benefit of such a chatbot is its ability to quickly provide information, often more efficiently than manual searches or navigating complex government websites. This pilot program could pave the way for wider adoption of AI-powered assistants in public services, ultimately making government interactions smoother and more user-friendly for everyone.

## 5. Google's AI Revolution: Bard Takes Center Stage

Google’s recent I/O conference was a showcase of its deep commitment to artificial intelligence, with a flurry of new AI-powered services being unveiled. From incredibly sophisticated automatic email writing to advanced photo editing, the company demonstrated how AI is being integrated across its product ecosystem. The highlight, however, was the significant advancements made to Google Bard, its AI-powered search assistant, indicating a strategic shift towards AI-first interactions.

Bard will now be powered by PaLM 2, the next generation of Google's advanced language model. PaLM 2 comes in four distinct versions (Gecko, Otter, Bison, Unicorn), each tailored for specific needs, from lightweight mobile applications to highly complex tasks. Google emphasizes PaLM 2's enhanced capabilities in mathematics and medicine, potentially allowing Bard to interpret medical images or debug complex code. This integration aims to make Bard a more powerful and versatile tool, fundamentally changing how users interact with information and complete tasks.

## 6. Bard's Global Rollout and Regulatory Hurdles

Following the Google I/O conference, Bard is now more widely accessible. A notable improvement is the elimination of the waiting list, allowing immediate access to the service in 180 countries, including English, Japanese, and Korean versions. This major expansion signifies Google’s intent to bring Bard to a massive audience, moving beyond its initial restricted beta phase.

However, despite this widespread rollout, Europe (including France) remains an exception. Regulatory hurdles, particularly concerning data privacy and AI governance, appear to be preventing Google from deploying Bard in any European country for the time being. This highlights the ongoing tension between rapid technological advancement and the need for robust ethical and legal frameworks, particularly in regions with stringent data protection laws like the EU. While users outside Europe can now experiment with Bard's enhanced capabilities, European users will have to wait until these regulatory issues are resolved.

## 7. The Nostalgic Comeback: BlackBerry's Story on Screen

A new film celebrating the iconic BlackBerry brand has just premiered in Canada, bringing a wave of nostalgia for a device that once dominated the mobile landscape. The movie chronicles the rise and fall of Research In Motion (RIM), the Canadian company behind the BlackBerry, from its humble beginnings in 1984 to its revolutionary impact in the late 1990s and early 2000s. The film captures the spirit of innovation and the eventual challenges that led to the device's decline.

BlackBerry devices were known for their physical QWERTY keyboards, secure messaging (BBM), and "push email" functionality, making them indispensable tools for business professionals and early adopters. Before the iPhone, BlackBerry was synonymous with mobile productivity and connectivity. The film is a reminder of the brand's cultural significance and its pioneering role in shaping smartphone technology. While not yet available globally, anticipation is high for this cinematic trip down memory lane, offering a look into the "startup era" of tech.

## 8. Edible Batteries: A Glimpse into Medical Tech's Future

In a fascinating innovation, researchers have reportedly developed edible batteries – batteries that can be safely ingested without harm. Italian scientists at the Italian Institute of Technology, with support from the European Research Council, have created tiny batteries made from everyday ingredients. These batteries include riboflavin (Vitamin B2) for the anode, quercetin (found in capers) for the cathode, seaweed as a separator, and beeswax with a touch of gold leaf for electrical contacts.

The primary application for these digestible batteries is in miniaturized electronic medical devices, which could be ingested for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes, offering a less invasive alternative to surgical procedures. Furthermore, these edible batteries address a critical safety concern: child accidental ingestion of button batteries, which currently poses significant health risks. While still a prototype, these batteries produce a low voltage (0.65 volts), making them safe for humans, and can generate up to 48 microamperes of current for about 12 minutes. This breakthrough could pave the way for a new era of digestible electronics.

## 9. TED Conference Highlights: AI, Climate, and Futurism

The recent TED conference in Vancouver was, unsurprisingly, heavily dominated by discussions around artificial intelligence and its profound implications. Speakers like Greg Brockman, co-founder of OpenAI, showcased advanced capabilities of ChatGPT, particularly its ability to integrate with various services like booking apps or ride-sharing, expanding its utility far beyond conversational AI. The potential for AI to autonomously create applications and solve complex problems was a key theme.

Another notable presentation focused on deepfakes and their real-time evolution. A developer demonstrated how AI can now alter a person's image and voice in real-time, highlighting both the technological advancement and the ethical dilemmas surrounding verifiable information. On the climate front, discussions included radical ideas like space-based solar energy, where massive solar farms in orbit would beam power wirelessly to Earth. While highly futuristic, these discussions underscore the relentless pursuit of innovative solutions for humanity's grand challenges, even if they remain decades away from practical implementation.

## 10. Sven: The TikTok of News for Young Audiences

Addressing the evolving media consumption habits of younger generations, a new French application called "Sven" aims to be the "TikTok of news" for users under 35. Sven is a video-first news aggregator that leverages the vertical video format and infinite scroll common to platforms like TikTok, but exclusively for high-quality news content. It partners with over 80 media outlets, ranging from general news (TF1, 20 Minutes) to specialized content (Explorateurs Médias for science, Mesdemoiselles for women's issues).

The app’s goal is to provide a curated, engaging news experience that resonates with digital natives. Sven also innovates in user interaction by allowing video comments, where users can record 10-second reactions to news stories, shared exclusively with their friends. Importantly, Sven actively combats the "filter bubble" effect common in social media by incorporating discovery and diversity into its recommendation algorithms. This ensures users are exposed to a wide range of perspectives and media, fostering critical thinking rather than reinforcing existing biases.
